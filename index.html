My Camino

After intense preparation, I headed to Spain 8 months after first watching The Way! I flew to Madrid, found my way to the right bus, and somehow managed to make it to my starting point without getting lost. I began my camino in Astorga, and ultimately walked 217 miles to Fisterre, which the early Romans believed to be the end of the world. I actually walked along the remarkably well-preserved remains of ancient Roman roads, through the clouds, and deftly avoided cowpies along the way. It was an amazing adventure and I cannot wait to do it again.

When to go

Depending on your preferences, the camino is wonderful any time of the year. This blog does a nice job explaining what to expect at different times throughout the year, including pilgrim traffic (which makes a difference when trying to find a bed in a hostel) and weather patterns.

Where to walk: So many choices!

There are several caminos to choose from ... click the arrow of stones picture for a link to the various camino routes available.

Each camino route includes its own delightful list of hostels to stay in. The best part about staying in hostels, aside from being a budget friendly choice (most hostels charge anywher3 from 3 to 7 euros per night -- some include meals), is how enhanced your camino experience becomes. You truly feel (and are) a pilgrim. You'll need to decide for yourself what your comfort level is, as most hostels feature multiple beds per room and you'll likely be surrounded by other pilgrims from all over the world.

To get a better idea of what hostel life is like, click here to learn more.
